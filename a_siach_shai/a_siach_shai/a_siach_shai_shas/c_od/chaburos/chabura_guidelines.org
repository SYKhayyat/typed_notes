Steps to writing a /chaburah/:
1. Picking a /sugya/: There are two types of sugyos which tend to hinder
one's ability to give a /chaburah/:
   1. There is typically one /lomdish/ point of the /sugya/ upon which a
/chabura/ is based. If this center is obscured by unfamiliar
/dinim/, it will be difficult to write a proper /chabura/; before
reaching the actual composition of the /chaburah/, much work is
necessary.
In addition, such a /chaburah/ will be, naturally, more difficult
to explain, for an explanation must cover those obscure /dinim/ in
addition to the /chaburah/.
   2. If that point is something that is already heavily discussed, it
will often be difficult to write a /chaburah/ about it. This is
because each thought one has will have to deal with a tremendous
amount of information.
2. Learning the relevant information. Relevant information includes the
/Gemara/, /Rashi/, and basic /Rishonim/. Obviously, there will
sometimes be others who are extremely relevant.
3. Thinking about the relevant information.
This should be done in conjunction with step two. Each point of the
/sugya/ that is learned deserves to be thought about before one
precedes to the next point. If one amasses the /sugya/, not thinking
it through until the end, two problems may occur:
   1. The points of the /sugya/ will probably not be clear enough to be
thought out. Learning a large amount of information without fully
digesting it tends to confuse the information; thinking about
points on which one lacks a clarity is rarely helpful.
   2. Many points will escape one's attention. If one only attempts to
sift through that which he has learned at the end, many points
will have migrated into his assumptions. He will then grant these
points without investigating them fully.
4. Organizing the conclusions. One should put together the information
gleaned from the second and third steps.
Points should be specifically classified- not just as “question” or
“answer,” but as “question on...” or “answer according to...for....”
In addition, points should be split into the basic question or
answer, and information necessary for that question or answer.
The purpose of a /chaburah/:
There are many benefits of the /chaburos/, both for the one giving the
/chaburah/ and for the listeners.
Benefits for the one giving the /chaburah/:
1. Obviously, one is learning; that is a benefit.
2. One trains his mind to learn /iyun,/ not in the context of regular
shiur. There are multiple advantages to this:
   1. The /iyun/ is made easier since any /sugya/- and any point of a
/sugya/- can be chosen.
   2. There will be no /shiur/ on the subject. Thus, one must be more
sure of his conclusions, because he will get no farther through
the /sugya/ than them.
3. One learns to publicly speak and explain what he understands. There
are two benefits to this:
   1. It is difficult to explain that which is but vaguely understood.
Thus, a need to explain forces a need to understand.
   2. Public speaking- both the shyness itself as well as the difficulty
of choosing the proper words to explain oneself while under
pressure- is a skill that will hopefully be used in the future,
and, therefore, needs practice.
4. One speaks and gets a /kesher/ with an older /bochur/- his /rosh/
/chaburah/- in learning. We older /bochurim/ have been around the
block a couple of times, and we have what to offer.
Benefits for those listening to the /chaburah/:
1. Obviously, there is the simple benefit of learning.
2. They are able to analyze their friend's /chaburah/. There are a few
benefits to this over regular /chazaras/ /hashiur/:
   1. Friends are less intimidating than /rebbeim/; it is easier to
discuss with them fine points.
   2. There are likely to be more difficult points to a friend's
/chaburah/ than to a /rebbi's/ /shiur/, since the /rebbi's/
/shiur/ is made by someone who has been learning and teaching for
years.
It is worthwhile, clearly, to keep these purposes in mind and to create
a /chaburah/ which has these gains. Thus, for example, a /chaburah/
which does not force one to “learn up” a /sugya/ by himself, or a
/chaburah/ which does not lend itself to discussion, should be avoided.
